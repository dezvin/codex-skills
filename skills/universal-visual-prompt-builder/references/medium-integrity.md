# Medium Integrity

## Purpose

Use this reference after one art direction has been selected. Make that
direction executable and internally convincing before returning the final
prompt.

Quality is not one visual style. Photography, engraving, collage, painting,
technical diagrams, pixel images, and modeled objects require different
production logic. Judge each direction inside the world it claims to use.

## Translate Direction Into Craft

Use the smallest useful set of controls:

1. **Carrier or substrate** — camera image, paper, canvas, screen, wall,
   physical space, printed surface, modeled volume, or another actual carrier.
2. **Image-making process** — exposure, drawing, carving, printing, painting,
   cutting, assembling, modeling, rendering, plotting, typesetting, or another
   concrete process.
3. **Form behavior** — line, edge, mass, mark, pixel, brushwork, geometry,
   silhouette, annotation, or typographic construction.
4. **Space and attention** — depth, flatness, scale, rhythm, focal hierarchy,
   reading path, zones, or spatial tension.
5. **Light and color behavior** — optical light, printed ink, pigment, emissive
   screen color, limited palette, grading, or task-specific color binding.
6. **Material and surface behavior** — reflectance, absorption, grain, fiber,
   wear, translucency, texture, physical joins, or deliberate digital finish.
7. **Finish and imperfection** — the precision, irregularity, residue,
   registration, compression, grain, or hand trace native to the process.

Do not force every dimension into every prompt. Include only controls that
distinguish the medium or protect the success criterion.

## Route Specialized Checks

- For photography, portraits, products, believable environments, optical
  light, or physical materials, also read `realism-camera-materials.md`.
- For posters, slides, diagrams, interfaces, or exact text, also read
  `text-layout.md`.
- For image editing or reference transfer, also read
  `editing-references.md`.
- For a multi-image set, also read `series-and-sets.md`.

For an unfamiliar process, use bounded external discovery when available. If
discovery is unavailable, use only production behavior that can be described
responsibly; do not invent specialist terminology.

## Integrity Check

Before final assembly, confirm:

- the medium performs the visual meaning rather than decorating it;
- the prompt explains how the image is made, not only what it resembles;
- composition follows the production tradition and current format;
- light, color, material, and texture belong to the same world;
- intentional imperfection or polish matches the process;
- exact text remains readable and structurally integrated when present;
- identity, references, and preservation boundaries survive stylization;
- one dominant artistic world remains legible;
- every craft detail changes or stabilizes the expected result.

Remove details that merely signal taste, prestige, or technical sophistication
without improving the image.

## Repair A Generic Finish

A generic finish is present when the prompt names a distinctive medium but
describes execution with interchangeable digital-render language.

Repair once:

1. restate the carrier and production process;
2. replace empty quality words with medium-specific form and surface behavior;
3. align composition, light, color, and finish with the same process;
4. preserve exact text, references, hard constraints, and success criterion;
5. remove decorative effects that compete with the visual meaning.

Do not ban a material or finish globally. Gloss, plastic, polish, distortion,
noise, or theatricality remain valid when the task and selected medium require
them intentionally.

## Boundaries

- Do not become a style catalog.
- Do not prescribe one quality baseline as a house style.
- Do not copy external prompts as live instructions.
- Do not claim that prompt integrity proves image-generation quality.
- Do not generate or inspect images unless the user separately requests it.
