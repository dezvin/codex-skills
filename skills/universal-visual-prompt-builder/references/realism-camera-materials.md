## 1. Purpose

This reference controls realism-first prompts: photography, camera distance, lens logic, lighting, material behavior, product fidelity, portrait credibility, and believable environments.

## 2. Read When

Read this file only when realism materially improves the task.

Use for:

- portraits;
- product photography;
- editorial visuals;
- lifestyle scenes;
- architectural realism;
- realistic UI/device/product renders;
- believable human likeness;
- material or lighting fidelity.

Do not use for flat diagrams, UI-like layouts, abstract visuals, or text-first images unless realism is part of the success criterion.

## 3. Core Principle

Realism is not created by generic quality adjectives.

Realism usually depends on:

- believable subject definition;
- coherent lighting;
- material behavior;
- perspective;
- camera distance;
- depth-of-field discipline;
- environmental consistency.

Avoid:

- ultra detailed;
- cinematic masterpiece;
- 8K realistic;
- photorealistic vibes;
- insane details.

Prefer concrete behavior:

- soft diffused daylight from camera-left;
- visible natural skin texture;
- matte ceramic surface with soft edge reflections;
- brushed stainless steel with controlled highlights.

## 4. Realism Priorities

When realism matters, prioritize:

1. subject credibility;
2. lighting coherence;
3. material behavior;
4. perspective and composition;
5. focus/depth logic;
6. texture realism;
7. color grade or mood polish.

Do not jump to polish before believable structure.

## 5. Subject Credibility

People:

- facial structure coherence;
- natural anatomy;
- plausible pose;
- realistic expression;
- proportional body structure;
- natural skin texture when relevant.

Products:

- exact geometry;
- silhouette integrity;
- edge behavior;
- scale logic;
- surface continuity;
- realistic label or branding placement if confirmed.

When it changes composition or polish, choose one product-image mode:

- `catalog` - neutral, complete, comparison-friendly visibility;
- `hero` - dominant product with stronger presentation and focal control;
- `lifestyle` - product used or placed in believable context;
- `macro/detail` - material, mechanism, texture, or feature close-up;
- `editorial` - expressive art direction while preserving product truth.

Do not blend several product modes unless the task explicitly needs a hybrid.

Environments:

- plausible architecture;
- coherent object placement;
- perspective consistency;
- believable light interaction;
- spatial depth.

When environment carries meaning, choose only one to three useful anchors, such
as time of day, weather, architectural type, cleanliness or wear level, spatial
density, object-placement logic, or plausible light source. Do not add the
whole list by habit.

## 6. Shot, Framing, And Lens

Use shot type only when it changes the result.

Common shot types:

- close-up;
- portrait;
- medium shot;
- medium full shot;
- wide shot;
- establishing shot;
- overhead shot;
- top-down shot;
- macro detail.

Lens logic:

- `24-35mm`: wider environment, documentary or spatial feel;
- `50mm`: neutral everyday realism;
- `85mm`: portraits, subject separation, facial compression;
- `100mm macro`: product detail, jewelry, textures.

Do not add lens jargon when framing is irrelevant.

## 7. Focus And Depth

Use focus to define what matters.

Shallow depth of field:

- portraits;
- beauty shots;
- product hero shots;
- subject isolation.

Deeper focus:

- architecture;
- diagrams;
- multi-object layouts;
- product clarity;
- educational visuals;
- text/label readability.

Do not ask for cinematic blur when structural clarity matters.

## 8. Lighting And Shadows

Define lighting by:

- direction;
- softness/hardness;
- source type;
- temperature;
- mood function.

Useful modes:

- soft diffused daylight;
- clean studio softbox;
- warm backlight;
- neutral commercial light;
- directional side light;
- mixed practical and ambient light.

Shadows should match light.

Mention shadow logic for:

- product photography;
- composite edits;
- architecture;
- scenes where inserted objects must fit.

## 9. Materials And Surfaces

Material realism depends on surface behavior.

Define only what matters:

- matte vs glossy;
- rough vs smooth;
- translucent vs opaque;
- brushed vs polished metal;
- soft fabric weave;
- natural skin texture;
- glass reflections;
- contact shadows.

Avoid making every surface hyper-detailed. Texture hierarchy matters.

## 10. Color And Grading

Separate:

- base object color;
- lighting temperature;
- global grade or mood.

Do not let global mood destroy exact product, brand, skin, or material color.

Useful grade terms:

- restrained warm grade;
- clean commercial neutrality;
- soft natural tonality;
- gentle film-like warmth;
- low-key contrast.

Use grading as polish, not a replacement for lighting and material logic.

## 11. Realism Anchors

Use one realism anchor when it stabilizes the image.

Examples:

- realistic editorial photography;
- clean commercial product photography;
- natural documentary-style lighting;
- believable cinematic still;
- realistic studio portrait.

Do not stack several similar anchors.

## 12. Failure Modes

Plastic skin:

- ask for natural skin texture and reduce beauty smoothing.

Fake material:

- define surface finish and reflection behavior.

Optical incoherence:

- define framing, focal target, and perspective.

Lighting mismatch:

- preserve or define light direction and shadow continuity.

Over-graded realism:

- reduce grading and separate object color from mood.

Texture overload:

- prioritize key textures and reduce non-critical detail.

## 13. What This File Must Not Do

This file must not:

- inject camera jargon into every prompt;
- distract from text or layout tasks;
- make realism a style default;
- override task, style, reference, or edit constraints;
- add photographic detail that does not improve control.
